How old are you, Thessaloniki?

The devastating earthquakes that hit Turkey and Syria at the beginning of February 2023 brought back to the public debate the question of how safe buildings are in our country. And while experts reassure the public considering how strict our anti-seismic regulation is, one can easily ask: how old are the buildings in Thessaloniki and what happens to those built before the first anti-seismic regulation, in 1959?

Open data on the construction year of the city's buildings is unfortunately not available. So we turned to... the classifieds. By meticulously collecting over 46,114 online home sales posted between 2015 and 2019, we were able to visualize the age distribution of the city's building infrastructure through the corresponding mark on Google Maps.

The graph below represents the locations of the shops within the city limits with their year of construction on the topographic map of Thessaloniki.

By zooming in on the years when the anti-seismic regulations were issued, both the pre-1959 buildings and how the city eventually expanded both east and west become clearer, while the historic center appears to include the oldest buildings as well.

The next step is to map the shops on the (nearest) building blocks of general and exclusive residential zones, based on the relevant topographic background of land uses of the geospatial information department of the Municipality of Thessaloniki. By comparing the location of each shop and the coordinates of the building blocks we can arrive at their estimated positions in the urban complex.

The graph below represents the average construction age for each building block. Toumba, New Switzerland, the Voulgari area, parts of the Upper Town, as well as the Kalhanokipoi include residences with the most modern versions of the anti-seismic regulations.

Extreme values in the sample can significantly affect the average value. Building blocks that include few old or just a few new properties show a skewed average price. For this purpose the median value can more realistically capture the age that is right in the middle of the sample. As it becomes evident, in the decades of the 60s and 70s the city was built rapidly, with these buildings being relatively newly built in the great Volvi earthquake of 1978.

It's also worth looking at when the oldest structure in each building block appears and how it compares to the landmark year 1959, when the country's first mandatory earthquake regulation was issued. In almost every neighborhood of the Historic Center, from Lefko Pyrgos and Aristotelous to Rotunda and Diokitirio, there appears to be at least one property that was built without any mandatory code.

Such properties are now more than 60 years old and may either lie abandoned in narrow alleys or still be used as residential or commercial premises. The behavior of these building facilities in a potentially strong seismic vibration, although it cannot be easily predicted, is certainly important to study in time.
